# Operation 'Eye of Sauron'

Long ago, in the deep heart of Germany, there stood an old city of stone and timber, weathered by rain, war, and the slow passing of years. Above its crooked streets rose a fortress, stern and grey, set upon a height. Its walls were not fair, nor adorned with the pride of kings, yet they had endured many winters and many troubles, and the people of that place trusted in them.

But beyond the walls, where the gardens ended and the dark wood began, there came a creeping menace. By day there was silence; by night there was movement. From holes beneath rotten boards, from broken sheds, and from the shadowed roots of trees, the marauders came forth. They did not bear swords, nor banners, nor the horns of war, yet they brought ruin in their own small way. They ravaged the fields of the humble folk, spoiled the flowers, gnawed what had been stored, and left behind them fear, filth, and anger.

At first the people endured it, as common folk often do. They mended what was broken, swept away the traces of the night, and muttered darkly over their losses. But the enemy returned, again and again, bold under cover of darkness. Then a small company of residents, weary of damage and delay, gathered together. They were not soldiers, but ordinary people driven by necessity; and at their head was an engineer, a maker of devices, patient of hand and sharp of mind.

So they set themselves to a strange labour. From wood, wire, glass, and metal they began to build a watchtower—not mighty as the towers of old legend, but tall enough to command the yard, the paths, and the dark places where the invaders crept. And half in jest, half in grim hope, they gave it a name out of ancient tales: the Tower of Sauron.

Upon its summit they fixed a single watching eye: not an eye of sorcery, but a lens of glass and craft, sleepless in the night. Beside it they placed a mechanism that could turn and follow movement in the gloom. And when the intruders came again beneath the stars, the Eye would wake, turn upon them, and send forth a cold and sudden beam—not to burn, nor to kill, but to startle, confuse, and drive them back into the shadows from which they had come.

Thus the tower rose, not as a weapon of war, but as a guardian of a small and troubled realm. And the people, seeing its dark shape against the evening sky, took heart; for though their fortress was old and their enemy lowly, they had not abandoned their home to the creatures of the night.
